I’ve always wanted a space to document the things I learn — somewhere I could easily revisit my notes without digging through random folders and files. Over time, I realised that instead of keeping all this information buried in a notepad, I might as well publish it. It not only gives me a cleaner way to organise what I learn, but also helps others who might be searching for the same thing one day. And that’s how this blog idea began. 

Initially, I thought of setting it up on WordPress or Blogger. A few of my friends were using those platforms, so they were the obvious choices. But after exploring them a bit, they just felt too heavy for what I wanted. My goal was simple — a personal space where I could write and publish without too much setup. All I really needed was a domain, a place to host my content, and something to turn Markdown into clean HTML. 

That’s when I came across [GitHub Pages and Jekyll](https://docs.github.com/en/pages/setting-up-a-github-pages-site-with-jekyll/creating-a-github-pages-site-with-jekyll/). It seemed like the perfect fit — free hosting, easy to maintain, and developer-friendly. But after a few hiccups installing Jekyll, I decided to look for alternatives and discovered [Hugo](https://gohugo.io/). Hugo is a static site generator written in Go, and it turned out to be exactly what I needed. You install it, write your content in Markdown, pick a [theme](https://themes.gohugo.io/) you like, and Hugo does the rest. For this site, I went with the [mini](https://themes.gohugo.io/themes/hugo-theme-cactus-plus/) theme because it’s simple, fast, and minimal. If you want to try it yourself, the [quick start guide](https://gohugo.io/getting-started/quick-start/) is an excellent way to begin.
